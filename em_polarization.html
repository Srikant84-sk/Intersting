1. Wave Polarization

Polarization describes the 2D geometric shape traced by the tip of the E-field vector in the transverse plane as the wave propagates along z. The two orthogonal components are:

Ex = Eox cos(ωt)    Ey = Eoy cos(ωt + Δφ)
  • Linear: Eoy = 0, or Eox = 0, or Δφ = nπ. Field oscillates on a fixed line.
  • Circular: Eox = Eoy AND Δφ = ±90°. Field traces a circle; axial ratio = 1.
  • Elliptical: General case. Axial ratio = semi-major/semi-minor axis.

Tilt Angle

τ = ½ arctan(2·Eox·Eoy·cos Δφ / (Eox² − Eoy²))

Axial Ratio (from coherency matrix eigenvalues)

D = √[(Eox²−Eoy²)² + 4·Eox²·Eoy²·cos²Δφ]
a = √[(Eox²+Eoy²+D)/2]   b = √[(Eox²+Eoy²−D)/2]
AR = a/b

2. Handedness (IEEE Convention)

Looking into the source: if the field rotates counterclockwise → RHCP (Right-Hand Circular Polarization). Clockwise → LHCP. Determined by sign of sin(Δφ): positive → RHCP.

3. Poynting Vector

S = E × H    [W/m²]

Direction: propagation direction (z-axis). Time-average power density:

Save = ½ Re(Es × Hs*)

For a plane wave in free space: Save = |E|² / (2η₀), where η₀ ≈ 377 Ω is the impedance of free space.

4. Polarization Loss Factor

Mismatch between transmit polarization ρ̂t and receive polarization ρ̂r causes power loss:

PLF = |ρ̂t · ρ̂r|²

Perfect alignment: PLF = 1. Orthogonal polarizations (e.g., RHCP → LHCP): PLF = 0 → complete rejection.
