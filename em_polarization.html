1. Wave Polarization: The "Shape" of the Field

In RF engineering and antenna design, efficient communication requires the transmitting and receiving antennas to be properly aligned. This alignment is dictated by polarization.

Polarization describes the 2D geometric shape traced by the tip of the Electric field vector (E) as the electromagnetic wave travels through space over time. Because a uniform plane wave travels in a single direction (e.g., along the z-axis), the electric field oscillates in the transverse plane (the xy-plane).

We define the polarization state by comparing the behavior of the x-component (Ex) and the y-component (Ey) of this field:

  • Linear Polarization: The electric field oscillates back and forth along a single straight line. This occurs if the wave has only one spatial component, or if the Ex and Ey components are perfectly in phase (Δφ = 0° or 180°). Standard AM radio and Wi-Fi dipole antennas typically utilize linear polarization.
  • Circular Polarization (CP): The tip of the electric field traces a perfect circle over time. For this to occur, two strict mathematical conditions must be met:
    • The amplitudes of the components must be identical (Eox = Eoy).
    • The phase difference between the components must be exactly 90° (Δφ = ±π/2).
    Note: FM radio, satellite communications, and GPS systems rely heavily on CP because it prevents signal loss when the receiver changes orientation.
  • Elliptical Polarization: This is the most common and general case in the real world. If the amplitudes are not perfectly equal, or if the phase difference is not exactly a multiple of 90° or 0°, the field traces an ellipse. In practice, most antennas designed to be circularly polarized exhibit slight elliptical polarization due to manufacturing imperfections.

2. The Poynting Vector: The "Power Pipe"

While polarization describes the geometry and orientation of the wave, the Poynting Vector (S) defines the actual power transported through space. It bridges the Electric (E) and Magnetic (H) fields to calculate the directional power density of the wave.

The instantaneous Poynting vector is defined by the cross product of the two fields:

S = E × H
  • Direction: The cross product mathematically ensures that S always points in the exact direction the wave is propagating.
  • Magnitude: Measured in Watts per square meter (W/m²), the magnitude calculates exactly how much power is intersecting a specific cross-sectional area (such as a receiving antenna).

Time-Average Power

Because the E and H fields oscillate at incredibly high frequencies (millions or billions of cycles per second), the instantaneous power fluctuates too rapidly to be a useful metric. Instead, engineers rely on the Time-Average Power Density (Save), which measures the continuous, usable energy delivered to a receiver over a full cycle:

Save = ½ Re(Es × Hs*)

(Where Es and Hs* represent the complex phasor forms of the electric and magnetic fields, and the asterisk denotes the complex conjugate).
